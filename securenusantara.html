Overlaps, Conflicts & Gaps
✅ Overlaps (Leverage Points)
Incident response procedures (ISO A.5.24 + Act 854 s.29 + NIST RS) share nearly identical control objectives. A single IR procedure satisfies all three. Similarly, access control (ISO A.5.15 + PDPA Security Principle + CSF PR.AA) can be unified under one policy framework.
⚠️ Conflicts
Data retention tension: ISO 27001 evidence retention (A.5.33) requires audit logs for ≥1 year. Act 854 may require longer retention for CNII incidents. PDPA's data minimization principle conflicts with long retention. Resolution: separate data classification — security logs ≠ personal data, treated differently.
🕳️ Gaps Identified
ISO 27001 does not mandate specific incident reporting timelines — Act 854's 6-hour window is more prescriptive. ISO's supply chain controls (A.5.19–A.5.22) don't fully cover third-party MSSP sub-processing under PDPA. Gap: formal Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with 3 sub-processors are missing.

💡 ISO 27001 Learning Point — Multi-Framework Strategy

A common mistake for organisations in Malaysia is treating ISO 27001, PDPA, and Act 854 as three separate compliance projects. SecureNusantara's approach — building ISO 27001 as the foundational layer with the others mapped onto it — is the right architecture. Every PDPA obligation can be traced to an ISO 27001 control. Every Act 854 incident reporting requirement is addressed in the IR procedure (A.5.24). This means SecureNusantara maintains one evidence repository that answers all three frameworks simultaneously.

What auditors look for: Cross-referencing. Can you show that your NACSA incident notification procedure (Act 854) is also the IR evidence for ISO 27001 A.5.24? Can you show that your PDPA data processing inventory is also your asset register for A.5.9? If yes — you have a unified compliance architecture. If not — you have three parallel programmes that will diverge over time.

Audit Defensibility — Beyond Checkbox Compliance
Consulting Insight

Most MSSP documentation fails audits not because documents don't exist, but because they can't demonstrate that documents are used. An auditor will ask: "Show me a recent incident where this IR procedure was followed." If the TheHive case doesn't reference the procedure version number, the evidence is weak.

SecureNusantara mitigation: all TheHive case templates include a mandatory field: "Procedure Version Applied." Shuffle playbooks embed the relevant SOP reference in their execution log. This creates a direct, auditable chain from event → tool action → documented procedure.

Evidence traceability is the difference between a document library and an ISMS.

Why PDCA matters for ISO 27001: ISO 27001 is not a one-time certification. It requires a functioning PDCA cycle that operates continuously. The certification audit evaluates whether the cycle is actually running — not just whether controls exist. The most common reason MSSPs fail re-certification (3-year surveillance audits) is that the ACT phase is neglected: non-conformities are closed but don't feed back into the risk register or policy updates. 💡 ISO 27001 Learning Point — Documented Information (Clause 7.5)

ISO 27001:2022 clause 7.5 requires organisations to create, update, and control documented information. The key test is: can you show an auditor not just that the document exists, but that it is current, approved, and actually used? The document pyramid represents this hierarchy. L5 Records are particularly important — they are the operational proof that controls are being applied day-to-day.

A practical tip from real implementations: Link every L3 Procedure to at least one L5 Record that proves it was followed. For example, SecureNusantara's IR Procedure (L3) is evidenced by TheHive case exports (L5). If you can't point to the evidence, the procedure is theoretical — and theoretically compliant is not compliant.

1. Purpose

This policy establishes the requirements for controlling access to SecureNusantara Sdn. Bhd. (SecureNusantara) information assets, systems, and client environments. It ensures that access is granted based on business need, maintained appropriately throughout employment, and revoked promptly upon change of role or termination.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all SecureNusantara employees, contractors, temporary staff, and third-party vendors who access SecureNusantara systems or client environments. It covers all information systems, including cloud platforms (AWS, Azure, GCP), on-premise SOC infrastructure, client monitoring portals, and productivity tools (Microsoft 365).

3. Policy Statements

3.1 Least Privilege: Access rights shall be granted at the minimum level required to perform job functions. Default access for new staff is read-only. Elevated permissions require manager approval and CISO review for privileged roles.

3.2 Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): All access rights shall be defined by role. Role definitions are maintained in the SecureNusantara RBAC Matrix (appendix to SN-POL-002). Individuals shall not accumulate access rights beyond their current role without formal approval.

3.3 Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA is mandatory for all accounts. Phishing-resistant MFA (hardware FIDO2 keys or Microsoft Authenticator number-matching) is required for all privileged accounts and client environment access. SMS-based OTP is not permitted for privileged access.

3.4 Privileged Access Management: All privileged access to production systems, client environments, and SOC infrastructure shall be managed via the approved PAM solution (HashiCorp Vault + Teleport). Session recording is mandatory for all privileged sessions. Shared accounts are prohibited except for designated breakglass accounts, which require dual-authorization and immediate post-use audit review.

3.5 Access Reviews: Access rights shall be reviewed quarterly for all users and monthly for privileged users. Reviews shall be conducted by the respective line manager and documented in the access review log (ACR-[YYYY-QN]). Accounts with no review within the review cycle shall be automatically suspended pending review.

3.6 Access Provisioning and Deprovisioning: New access requests require submission via IT ServiceDesk with manager approval. All access shall be revoked within 4 hours of termination notification for involuntary departures and within 24 hours for voluntary departures. IT ServiceDesk generates an automated offboarding ticket upon HR system update.

3.7 Remote Access: Remote access to SecureNusantara systems requires VPN (WireGuard enterprise) with MFA. Client environment access via remote protocols (RDP, SSH) must be proxied through Teleport. Direct RDP/SSH to client systems is prohibited.

3.8 Guest and Visitor Access: Guest network access is provided on a segregated VLAN with no access to internal systems. Visitor access to the SOC floor requires host escort and visitor register entry at all times.

4. Exceptions

Exceptions to this policy require written approval from the CISO with documented business justification, risk assessment, and compensating controls. Exceptions are time-limited (maximum 90 days) and subject to mandatory review. All approved exceptions are recorded in the Policy Exception Register (PER-[YYYY]).

5. Non-Compliance

Violations of this policy may result in immediate access revocation, disciplinary action up to and including termination of employment, and/or legal action where applicable under Malaysian law. Security incidents arising from policy non-compliance will be investigated under the Incident Response Procedure (SN-PROC-002).

Critical Thinking & Challenge Layer
Mandatory counterpoints, assumptions challenged, and hard trade-offs

Challenge 1: Is ISO 27001 certification enough to signal MSSP trustworthiness?

Honest answer: No. ISO 27001 certifies that you have a documented, systematic ISMS. It does not certify that you are secure. SIRIM QAS auditors spend roughly 3–5 days assessing an organization. They cannot evaluate the quality of 48,000 daily alerts, the judgement of a tired Tier 1 analyst at 3AM, or whether your Wazuh rules are actually tuned well. The certificate tells clients that processes exist. It says nothing about whether adversaries fear your SOC.

Alternative framing: SOC 2 Type II (common in US market, increasingly referenced by Malaysian tech companies) is time-based — it certifies that controls worked over a 6–12 month observation period, not just on the audit day. ISO 27001 has no such temporal evidence requirement. A hybrid approach — ISO 27001 for Malaysian market trust + SOC 2 Type II for US-connected clients — is commercially superior but operationally demanding and expensive.

Challenge 2: Compliance ≠ Security. How does SecureNusantara prevent the "certificate theatre" failure mode?

The most dangerous outcome is achieving ISO 27001 certification and then treating it as done. Evidence from real MSSP incidents: several certified MSSPs have suffered breaches post-certification because certification was used to satisfy client procurement requirements, not to actually drive security improvement.

SecureNusantara mitigation strategy: embed ISO 27001 controls into operational tools (Wazuh, TheHive, Shuffle) rather than maintaining a parallel paper ISMS. When the control is the process and the tool is the evidence generator, the gap between "certified" and "secure" narrows. But this only works if Shuffle playbooks are actually deployed — which, at 40% deployment rate, is currently not the case.

Hard truth: If SecureNusantara achieves certification with 40% of planned SOAR automation deployed, the certificate will be describing a security posture that doesn't fully exist yet. This is not dishonest — it is common — but leadership must understand this gap.

Challenge 3: Open-source stack — cost-effective or operationally risky?

Wazuh, MISP, OpenCTI, TheHive, and Shuffle are all powerful tools. They are also tools that require significant internal expertise to operate, tune, and maintain. SecureNusantara has no commercial support contract for any of these tools. When Wazuh releases a major update, the internal engineering team must assess, test, and deploy it — against a backlog of other priorities. The risk: a critical Wazuh vulnerability (there have been several) creates a patch urgency that competes with client deliverables. Commercial SIEM vendors provide support SLAs; open source provides control but no safety net.

The budget reality counterpoint: A commercial SIEM (Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel, QRadar) would cost SecureNusantara MYR 800K–2M annually at their current log volume. The open-source stack costs roughly MYR 180K/year in engineering time and infrastructure. The delta is real money. For an MYR 18M revenue company, commercial SIEM is not viable without a major pricing restructure. The open-source bet is financially rational — but the risk must be consciously managed, not ignored.

Challenge 4: The 6-month certification timeline — is it realistic?

Six months is aggressive. ISO 27001 implementations at MSSPs of this size typically take 9–14 months end-to-end. The 6-month timeline is being driven by commercial pressure (client contract renewals) rather than implementation readiness. Risk: if Stage 1 audit reveals scope definition issues or multiple major NCs, the timeline collapses and client relationships are at risk anyway. A 6-month timeline succeeds only if: (a) leadership is fully engaged, (b) GRC team has dedicated bandwidth (currently split with client advisory work — a conflict), (c) all major NCs are resolved before Stage 2 (NC-001 and supplier DPAs are current threats to this), and (d) no significant security incidents occur during the implementation period that require all-hands focus.

Recommendation: Be transparent with the 3 clients whose contract renewals depend on this. A 7-8 month timeline with high quality evidence is better than a 6-month rush that fails Stage 2 audit and results in a 4-month delay anyway. This is a conversation the vCISO must have with the CEO — and it will be uncomfortable.

Challenge 5: PDPA and Act 854 — are they actually enforced in Malaysia?

A pragmatic question. As of 2024, PDPA enforcement has been historically light — the PDPC has issued relatively few fines compared to GDPR-equivalent authorities in Europe. This creates moral hazard: organizations may underinvest in PDPA compliance because enforcement risk is perceived as low. Act 854 (Cyber Security Act 2024) is newer and NACSA's enforcement posture is still developing. However, two trends are shifting this calculus: (1) Act 854 includes criminal liability for company officers, which changes the risk calculus entirely from organizational to personal; (2) regional harmonization with ASEAN data protection frameworks is increasing pressure on PDPC to be more active. SecureNusantara's GRC team should not build compliance programs around current enforcement laxity. The regulatory trajectory is clearly toward stricter enforcement, and SecureNusantara manages data for clients who face direct regulatory scrutiny themselves.

Challenge 6: Staffing — the ISO 27001 implementation is cannibalizing operational capacity

Every hour the GRC Manager spends on ISO documentation is an hour not spent on client advisory work. Every hour a Tier 3 analyst spends preparing audit evidence is an hour not spent on threat hunting. This is a real operational cost that is invisible in the implementation budget. Conservative estimate: the ISO 27001 project is consuming 25–30% of GRC team capacity and 10–15% of senior SOC analyst capacity for 6 months. At SecureNusantara staffing levels, this is the equivalent of 1.5–2 FTE for 6 months — a cost of approximately MYR 90K–120K that is not captured in the project budget. If this is not accounted for, client service quality degrades, SLA compliance drops, and the certification that was supposed to win new business is achieved at the cost of losing existing business.
